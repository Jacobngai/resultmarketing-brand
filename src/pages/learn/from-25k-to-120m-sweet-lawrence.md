## The $25,000 Bet

> "I couldn't eat what most bakeries were selling anymore. It gave me that sugar-high sugar crash. So I started making my own recipes."

Not 500 stores. Not nationwide. *One.*

Here's what most entrepreneurs miss: Lauren stayed small on purpose.

> "I got into one store. I demoed in that Whole Foods every week to make sure it was selling. I kept the business really quite small for 1-2 years until I really found product-market fit."

## The Data That Changed Everything

After two years of grinding, Sweet Lawrence got into 5,000+ stores nationwide (Publix, Kroger). That's when the emails started flooding in:

> "I love your cookie dough but my husband is gluten-free. My kid has a nut allergy. My sister can't eat dairy. Can you please create something for them?"

She reformulated everything â€” free of ALL top allergens. Gluten, dairy, egg, nuts, soy, sesame, coconut.

The result?

> "Within six months, it was our #1 SKU."

But here's the insight that matters:

> "50% of our customers had NEVER shopped the cookie dough category before. We weren't taking market share from the big guys. We brought NEW customers who were just walking right by."

She didn't compete. She created a new category.

## Don't Scream Your Niche

After going all-in on allergen-free, Lauren made a counterintuitive packaging decision:

> "We made 'gluten-free' much smaller on the package. We're not screaming it â€” because that's not our reason for being. We wanted to look like a damn good, delicious, modern cookie."

Why? Screaming "gluten-free" alienates people WITHOUT allergies. But they might ALSO want cleaner ingredients.

The lesson: Your niche is your superpower, but it doesn't need to be your headline.
